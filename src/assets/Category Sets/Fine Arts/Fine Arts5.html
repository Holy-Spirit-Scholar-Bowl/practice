1. Fine Arts/All (PACE NSC 2006)
Alexander Scriabin considered it “golden,” while Baroque and Classical composers called it “the key of glory,” and works requiring trumpets were written in it. It is the most frequently used key in the keyboard sonatas of Domenico Scarlatti, and often requires clarinets in B-flat to switch to clarinets in A. The violin concertos of Beethoven, Brahms, and Tchaikovsky are all set in this key, also found in many symphonies of Haydn and Mozart. For 10 points, name this key with two sharps whose relative minor is B minor.
ANSWER: D major

3. Fine Arts/All (WUHSAC XI 2009)
His “O How Amiable” for choir and organ concludes with a quotation of the hymn tune “St. Anne”. 9. Fine Arts/All (PACE NSC 2005)
While the Roman cavalry used trumpets, the infantry used this instrument for the same purposes. The Bulgarian gaida and the East European dudy are equivalents that incorporate cowbells, while France’s cornemuse is the traditional accompaniment of the hurdy-gurdy or the bombarde at Christmas. The archetypical classical kind, the “piobroch,” was created by Iain Odhar and the MacCrimmon family. For 10 points, name this musical instrument whose distinguished sound is produced by the contrast of a chanter and several drones, similar to the Irish “union pipe” but originating in Scotland.
ANSWER: bagpipes

13. Fine Arts/All (Delta Burke 2003)
Five out of this artist’s eight sons became painters and his brother, Dirk, was a skilled painter of interior scenes. He was among the first to use alla prima, the technique of painting directly on a canvas. Spending most of his later years in financial troubles his compositions became more dour as evidenced by The Women Regents of the Old Men’s Home at Haarlem. He was a master of group portraiture and with instinctive and rapid brushstrokes was adept at capturing momentary facial expressions. FTP name this Dutch portraitist of The Archers of Saint Hadrian and The Laughing Cavalier.
ANSWER: Franz Hals.
